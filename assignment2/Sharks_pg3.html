Bull Sharks

If any shark is to earn its reputation for being a vicious killing machine,
the bull shark would be at the top of the list. While it is much smaller than
some sharks (the largest recorded specimen was a female about 8 feet long and
over 800 lbs, but average females usually measure 8 feet and 300 lbs.), the level
of aggression in the bull shark is high, and their behavior is considered unpredictable
by many scientists. Their aggressive nature and stocky build is where the bull
shark gets its name, but it is also known by many other local names such as the
zambi shark or the river shark. Bull sharks are especially unique and potentially
more dangerous because of their adaptability. While most other sharks are bound
to the salinated waters of the seas, bull sharks are fully capable of surviving far
inland in fresh water habitats such as rivers and lakes. They also tend to prefer
murky waters, making it hard to tell if one is around until it is too late. Despite this
seeming advantage to survival, bull sharks are still considered to have a population
that is threatened globally, and they fall under the vulnerable classification
which is the last step before becoming endangered. It is important to protect what
remains of the bull shark population and try to build it back to stability.
