Tiger Sharks

Named after its recognizable tiger stripes, the tiger shark is another well known shark that is an apex predator like the great white, though not quite measuring up to the same size. Average tiger sharks measure between 11-14 feet, and they weigh anywhere from 500-1500 lbs. Like most sharks, female tiger sharks outsize the males, with the largest recorded female coming in at 16 feet long and weighing over 2000 lbs. Like the great white, orca pods are the only known predator to tiger sharks and even so it is not a common occurrence. Tiger sharks are often referred to as the garbage cans of the sea because they are not picky about what they eat, and because of the shape and serrations of their teeth they can eat harder prey such as sea turtles without encountering too much difficulty with the exterior shell. Tiger sharks are also one of the few sharks who will actually eat a human as opposed to just biting them, making them more dangerous in the case of an attack. Even so, attacks from tiger sharks are rare and the sharks are often safely worked with by scientists. The females tend to be more curious and friendly than the males, and tiger sharks are often the sharks that are around for tourist adventures such as shark cage diving or diving with sharks. Part of the reason for this is location, tiger sharks tend to stay near the coast in tropical waters, preferring warmth and following the warm currents during the colder months. While tiger sharks do not necessarily live in packs, there has been evidence of them working together in certain situations, such as an island off the coast of Australia having so many tiger sharks that they actually top the food chain over great whites in the area, despite the natural tendency and size advantage going to the great white. For the most part though, they are somewhat solitary creatures despite their numbers.
