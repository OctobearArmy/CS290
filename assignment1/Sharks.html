Introduction

Sharks are one of a kind in more than one way! Although they are fish, many sharks give birth to
live young. This separates them from other fish who lay eggs to reproduce. Some species of sharks
have even more unique reproductive practices, such as sand tiger sharks having two wombs to ensure
that two pups survive the pregnancy. Female sharks across the board are considered stronger and tougher
than the males, partially because of how aggressive most sharks are when they breed. In order to survive,
the female sharks must endure bites and other aggressive behavior from the males. But don’t worry!
Most sharks take a long nap afterwards, upwards of 12 hours.


Sharks come in all shapes and sizes, and eat all sorts of food. Some sharks are huge and eat tiny food,
like whale sharks and basking sharks. Despite being the largest sharks, they eat only small prey like krill.
Other sharks like great whites prefer to eat bigger prey like seals or other smaller sharks and fish.
Still other sharks like the tiger shark are not picky with what they eat, earning them the nickname of
the trash can of the sea. They have even adapted to easily devour sea turtles despite their protective shell.
Bull sharks are another well known hungry shark, they oftentimes will work their way upstream into the freshwater
to hunt for easy food.

Important Shark Terms

Ampullae
- sensory structures located on a shark’s body that detect electrical impulses over a short range
Dermal Denticles
- aka “skin teeth;” the protective (placoid) scales that cover a sharks body
Tapetum lucidum
- a tissue present in shark eyes, helps reflect light to the retina and increase their visibility in dark waters.

Why sharks are our friends.

As vast as the ocean is, it takes a lot of creatures to keep everything in balance.
One such creature is the shark, with species as varied as they are unique. There are big sharks and
small sharks, and sharks in the deep water and sharks in the shallows. There are sharks everywhere in
between. And without these sharks the ecosystem would be out of whack, and in some place so would the
economy! It is important that we try and protect these sharks from mass culling from fishermen’s nets,
shark finning practices, and fear induced shark hunting practices. Statistically, humans kill far more
sharks than shark have ever killed humans.
